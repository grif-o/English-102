Homepage Introduction

The Greatest Marketing Campaign in American History Was Built on a Lie

In 1964 the US Surgeon General released a report that confirmed what medical researchers had been warning about for some time. This was the adverse negative effects caused by cigarette consumption. As we look at health guidelines today, it would make you think that this was the end of the cigarette, but no it was just the beginning of the fight between tobacco companies and medical findings. As these guidelines came out the tobacco industry had already set itself up to create doubt among the findings of these studies to keep the public interested and consuming their product. This was among many of the tactics used by the many companies to keep profits up, and doubt down about their products.

This exhibit focuses on the 1960s as an important time in the culture of America and public health. A moment when the tobacco industry was faced with growing knowledge of the negative effects associated with the consumption of their products. However they responded to this not honestly, but with mass manipulation of public opinion that had never been seen before. These companies started to play offense by funding their own studies and pushing the idea of cigarettes as American and masculine. Creating public uncertainty when it came to the drawbacks and pushing the positives, this was quite the interesting advertising campaign.

The artifacts in this exhibit, many of them Marlboro archives, bring the cultural side of the marketing strategy used. These advertisements didn't sell cigarettes, they sold the brand identity of something people wanted to be associated with at the time. Through the cowboys and sense that Americans smoke cigarettes we see how they impacted public opinion and with the manipulation of scientific studies to create manufactured findings, you start to get an idea of the campaign that these companies were running. With the idea of smoking being normal, and that it wasn't dangerous, this led many people to fall into addiction to cigarettes and tobacco products.

This exhibit invites you to look at how the tobacco industry in the 1960s was more than a simple story of corporate greed, and goes deeper into how public opinion can be swayed through the use of manufactured science and cultural norms being engendered. All of this at the same time and done by design to create an addiction prone country. This is a lesson that sometimes you need to peel back the layers of the onion to question the things that are being pushed as normal based on questionable scientific findings, in this case it was the research, and dishonorable corporate interest.
